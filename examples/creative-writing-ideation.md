# Worked Example — Creative Writing Ideation

**Prompt**: "Narrative arcs for a sci-fi short story about humanoid robot ethics."

---

## Phase 1 — Frame

```
## Problem Statement
A short story (~5,000 words) exploring ethical questions raised by humanoid robots.
Must avoid Asimov / Black Mirror tropes. Should leave the reader with a productive
ethical discomfort rather than an answer. Voice and register are open.

## Domain
creative
```

## Phase 2 — Reframe

```
## Reframes
1. (False Faces) "Robot ethics is dramatic" reversed → robot ethics is BORING — the
   most ethically interesting questions emerge in mundane maintenance schedules,
   warranty terms, or warranty-expired robots in landfills.
2. (True/False) Premise "the robot is the protagonist" is assumed → flip: the robot
   is a minor character whose ethics-question is incidental to a human-centered
   plot.
3. (Slice and Dice / role) Decompose by who has the ethical question — the robot,
   the owner, the manufacturer, a child who grew up with one, an insurance adjuster,
   a grieving widow whose late husband had a robot lookalike.

**Selected reframe(s):** #1 (boring ethics) — most divergent; trope-resistant by
construction.
```

## Phase 3 — Diverge

```
**Seed:** 1271 (mod 5 = 1; favorites = EXCLUDED)
**Selected frameworks:** hall-of-fame [semantic], brutethink [random-stimulus, word: "compost"],
inversion [inversion], three-bs [temporal — wildcard], think-bubbles [structural]
```

### Hall of Fame
Borrowed perspectives (eras + dispositions):
1. **Anton Chekhov** (19th c. Russian playwright): the ethical moment in robot disposal as a domestic scene; no hero, no villain, only the texture of a quiet afternoon
2. **Octavia Butler**: a robot maintenance worker (working class) whose ethical clarity exposes the comfortable rationalizations of robot owners
3. **Kobo Abe**: a Kafkaesque slow bureaucratic horror — the robot's warranty form contains a mistranslation that has accidentally granted it personhood; nobody can rescind it
4. **Eve Babitz** (LA scenester): a hot-and-haze short about three women who own the same model robot, in the same building, with very different feelings about him
5. A pre-1900 figure (Chuang Tzu): is the dreamer the dream, the owner the owned, in a robot context? a parable, not a plot

### Brutethink (compost)
Properties: decay, layering, microbial activity, finished product, smell, time, gardener-as-tender.
1. Composting robot: the story is about a future where retired robots are interred in gardens to "compost" their components and personhood-data
2. Layering: temporally layered narrative — same robot, three owners across 50 years; each ethical stance becomes mulch for the next
3. Microbial activity: the robot's ethics emerge not from its programming but from the bacterial colonies that have grown in its hollow joints — accidental sentience via neglect
4. Smell: the visceral; a smell-centered narrative where what makes the robot human is its smell after years of co-habitation

### Inversion
"How would we GUARANTEE the worst possible robot-ethics short story?"
- Make it heavy-handed; have characters say the moral aloud
- Use established tropes uncritically
- Set in a future-Tokyo-with-neon
- Include a sentient robot who explicitly questions its servitude
- Have a tragic ending where someone learns a lesson

Inverses (idea pool):
1. The moral never spoken; only acted
2. Trope INVERSION as the core mechanic — the robot is what we expected the human to be
3. Setting in a deeply mundane place (a Phoenix retirement community; a Kentucky truck stop)
4. The robot DOESN'T question its servitude; it's the human who is uneasy
5. Open-ended ending; no lesson learned; just consequence

### Three Bs
After loading the prompt for 15 min, walked. Captured ideas:
1. "The widow keeps polishing him long after he's stopped working. The kids call it grief. She calls it Tuesday."
2. "The estate-sale rug auctioneer realizes the robot is also being auctioned, and the warranty transfer is more legally interesting than anything else in the lot."
3. "Two robot-repair-people in love; their argument is whether to honor the manufacturer's reset request when a unit comes back too many times."

### Think-Bubbles
Branches: setting, narrator, time, mood, conflict.
Cross-jumps:
1. (setting × time) a Kentucky truck stop, 2071 — robot-trucker labor disputes seen through the night-shift waitress's POV; her ethics matter more than the robots'
2. (narrator × mood) child narrator + restrained mood — what does a robot owner's child notice that adults don't?
3. (conflict × setting) a quiet suburban neighborhood + an HOA dispute about robot lawn-care equipment that escalates

## Phase 4 — Cross-pollinate

Hybrids:
1. **The Composted Repair-Couple** — Brutethink #1 + Three-Bs #3 = a married pair of robot-repair workers who argue about a unit returned too many times; their resolution involves a quiet ceremony of "composting" the unit in their backyard. Domestic ethics.
2. **HOA Auctioneer's Lot** — Three-Bs #2 + Think-Bubbles #3 = an HOA dispute about a robot leads to an estate-sale auction where warranty transfer reveals decades of micro-rationalizations across owners.
3. **Babitz-on-Bourbon** — Hall-of-Fame Eve Babitz + Brutethink "smell" = three women in an LA building who all own the same robot model; one of them finally smells him and realizes he's been with her longer than her dead husband.
4. **Chuang Tzu in Phoenix** — Hall-of-Fame Chuang Tzu + Inversion "deeply mundane setting" = a 78-year-old woman in a Phoenix retirement community has dreams she doesn't fully understand; her caretaker is a robot. The ethical question is whose dreams these are.
5. **Maintenance-Layered Storm** — Brutethink "layering" + Hall-of-Fame Octavia Butler = same robot, three owners across 50 years, told from the perspective of the maintenance worker who serviced him for all three. The maintenance worker is the moral compass.

Provocation: "What if the story has no robot in it at all, only its absence?"
- A short about the disposal-pickup truck driver who collects retired robots; he never meets them living. The ethics live in his careful protocol.

## Phase 5 — Surface

```
## Curated Ideas (narrative arcs)

### Direction A — Domestic & Mundane
1. **The Composted Repair-Couple** — married pair argue over reset; quiet backyard
   ceremony resolves it. [hybrid #1]
2. **HOA Auctioneer's Lot** — HOA dispute → estate auction → warranty-history
   reveal. [hybrid #2]

### Direction B — Maintenance Worker as Moral Compass
3. **Maintenance-Layered Storm** — same robot, 50 years, 3 owners, told by his
   serviceman. [hybrid #5]
4. **The Disposal-Truck Driver** — robot ethics seen through the cold protocol
   of retirement pickup. [provocation]

### Direction C — Ambient & Lyrical
5. **Babitz-on-Bourbon** — three women, one robot model, the smell-recognition
   moment. [hybrid #3]

### Direction D — Philosophical Quiet
6. **Chuang Tzu in Phoenix** — retirement community, dreams, robot caretaker;
   the question of whose dreams. [hybrid #4]

### Loonshot Candidates
1. **The Composting Garden** — a future where retired robots are interred in
   gardens to "compost" their components and personhood-data; a story set
   among the gardeners.
   Looks fragile: weird premise; readers may bounce off the imagery |
   Asymmetric upside: an entire visual & emotional vocabulary that no robot-
   ethics story has used; could become canonical if the writing lands.
   [Brutethink #1]
2. **The Bureaucratic Personhood** — a robot whose warranty form contains a
   mistranslation that accidentally granted it personhood; nobody can rescind
   it. The story is the bureaucracy.
   Looks fragile: comic register may undermine ethical weight |
   Asymmetric upside: the funniest entry in robot-ethics literature, AND a
   genuine probe into how rights are constructed; cross-quadrant appeal.
   [Hall-of-Fame Kobo Abe #3]

### Notes
- Reframe most generative: #1 (boring ethics) — explicitly trope-resistant.
- Frameworks contributing most: Hall of Fame and Brutethink.
- Dropped on devil's advocate: "robot questions servitude" arc (Inversion #4
  pointed AWAY from this trope; we honored the inverse) and "future-Tokyo-with-neon"
  (deliberately rejected as cliché trigger).
```
