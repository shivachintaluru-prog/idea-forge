## When to use
Generate ideas that are deliberately, aggressively BAD. Then mine them for the kernel of value buried inside. The "permission to be bad" defeats the convergent quality-filter; the kernel-mining step recovers value from the surface absurdity. Best for problems where the convergent quality bar is causing hidden mode collapse.

## The technique
1. Clearly state the problem you want to solve.
2. Generate 10-15 deliberately TERRIBLE ideas — solutions that would be embarrassing, unethical (in mild ways), absurd, or obviously failure-bound. Aim for genuinely bad, not "ironically bad."
3. For each terrible idea, ask: "What's actually TRUE about this idea? What unmet need or buried insight does it expose?"
4. The exposed insights become the seed for legitimate ideas. The legitimate idea is OFTEN A REFRAMING of the terrible one — the SAME insight, in a non-terrible form.
5. Write the legitimate-form-of-the-terrible-idea as the harvest.

The technique works because terrible ideas often have the right INTUITION but the wrong FORM. By generating terrible ideas freely, you access intuitions that the quality filter would have blocked.

## Anti-mode-collapse prompts
- The terrible ideas must be GENUINELY terrible — not "slightly wrong" but "obviously bad." Self-censorship turns the technique into mode collapse with extra steps.
- For every terrible idea, you must extract at least one TRUTH or INSIGHT — even from the worst. If you can't find one, the idea wasn't terrible enough (it was just wrong).
- The legitimate form must preserve the INSIGHT, not the form. If your "legitimate" version still has the terrible-form's worst element, redo.

## Example application (synthetic)
Problem: "Reduce bias in hiring decisions."

Terrible ideas:
1. Hire only people who match the existing team's astrological signs.
2. Reverse the resume — hire only applicants whose resumes are blank.
3. Make the hiring decision via a 5-second gut reaction.
4. Pay candidates to lie on their resume; hire those whose lies are most plausible.
5. The CEO personally vetoes any hire that "feels too qualified."
6. Hire by reverse-auction: each hire bids how little they're willing to be paid.

Insights extracted:
- (1) Bias-via-pattern-matching is the actual problem; even "good fit" criteria smuggle bias.
- (2) Resumes carry historical bias signal; experiments with blind / structured-only review surface buried talent.
- (3) Snap judgments are systematically biased; structured 30-min decision protocols are not.
- (4) The ABILITY TO TELL A COMPELLING STORY is being unconsciously evaluated above competence.
- (6) Compensation-anchoring is a real bias source; budget transparency may help.

Legitimate-form ideas:
- Blind first-round review where no biographical info is visible
- 30-min structured decision protocol with explicit veto points
- Compensation transparency at the job description level (specific number, no negotiation)
- Resume "factoring" — the resume is split into competence claims and biographical context, only the former goes into the first round

## Output format
- 10-15 terrible ideas
- Insights extracted per idea
- 5-8 legitimate-form harvest ideas
