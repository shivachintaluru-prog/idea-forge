## When to use
Borrow the perspective of distinct figures (real, fictional, historical) and apply each to the problem. The variation in worldview produces genuinely different ideas — each "hat" is a different optimization function.

## The technique
1. Pick 5 figures from VERY different eras, fields, and dispositions. Enforce the spread:
   - 1 from before 1900
   - 1 from a non-business field (artist, scientist, athlete, religious figure)
   - 1 from a culture/region you're less familiar with
   - 1 fictional character
   - 1 contemporary contrarian (someone whose public position you disagree with)
2. For each figure, ask: "How would they approach this problem? What would they refuse to do? What would they obsess over?"
3. Generate 1-2 ideas per figure, framed in their voice/values.

## Anti-mode-collapse prompts
- Reject all current celebrities and the obvious "innovator" picks — present-day tech CEOs whose perspectives are already over-represented in training data.
- The contrarian pick must be someone you disagree with — that's where the friction generates novel ideas.
- For the pre-1900 figure, lean into their actual constraints (no electricity, no internet, no globalization). The forced anachronism breaks assumptions.

## Example application (synthetic)
Problem: "Make corporate all-hands meetings more engaging."

- **Hildegard von Bingen** (12th-c. mystic composer): Replace slides with chant; the all-hands begins with 60 seconds of held silence.
- **Maria Montessori**: Self-directed stations. People rotate through topics at their own pace, no fixed plenary.
- **Eiji Toyoda** (TPS): The "all-hands" is a 5-minute standup at the line where the work happens; status flows up via genchi-genbutsu.
- **Marcus Aurelius**: Pre-meeting memento mori — a 90-second reflection on what would actually matter if the company didn't exist.
- A contrarian who thinks meetings should be longer, not shorter: design a 3-hour all-hands with built-in breaks; the duration creates space for unexpected encounters.

## Output format
- 5 figures, each with a 1-2 sentence idea attributed to their worldview
