## When to use
Mind-mapping done with explicit cross-branch connections. The branches alone are mode collapse; the *cross-jumps between distant branches* are where genuinely new ideas live.

## The technique
1. Place the central concept (the problem) in the middle.
2. Draw 4-6 first-order branches — major dimensions of the problem (e.g., motivation, time, resources, social context, identity, regulation).
3. From each first-order branch, expand 3-4 second-order branches with specific elements.
4. **Critical step**: identify CROSS-BRANCH connections. Pairs of second-order nodes from DIFFERENT first-order branches that, when combined, suggest an idea neither branch alone produced. Force at least 3 cross-jumps.
5. Each cross-jump generates 1 idea.

The branches map the territory; the cross-jumps are what you came for.

## Anti-mode-collapse prompts
- The branches themselves are NOT ideas. Resist the urge to harvest single-branch nodes as outputs — those are just labels.
- For cross-jumps, prefer pairs whose first-order parents are SEMANTICALLY DISTANT. (Motivation × Time ≫ Resources × Tools.)
- If your first 3 cross-jumps cluster on one parent pair, add a fourth from a different pair.
- Force at least 1 cross-jump that initially seems irrelevant. The "irrelevant" jumps are where the surprise lives.

## Example application (synthetic)
Central: "Learning a new language."

Branches: Motivation, Time, Resources, Social, Identity, Memory.

Second-order under Motivation: external-pressure, intrinsic-curiosity, future-utility, embarrassment-avoidance.
Second-order under Memory: spaced-repetition, episodic-anchor, body-memory, dream-state.

Cross-jump (Motivation × Memory):
- "embarrassment-avoidance × episodic-anchor": each lesson is anchored to a specific embarrassing situation the learner has experienced. The shame-memory becomes the retrieval cue.
- "intrinsic-curiosity × dream-state": pre-sleep micro-lessons that intentionally use the language for self-talk in the moments before sleep, hijacking dream rehearsal.

Both cross-jumps produce ideas not present in any single branch.

## Output format
- The central node + first/second-order branch structure
- ≥3 cross-jump ideas, each tagged with the two parent branches
