## When to use
Ideas don't die randomly — they die at predictable inflection points. Mapping the "three deaths" of an idea reveals where to intervene and which ideas have escape routes built in.

## The technique
For each candidate idea in your pool, walk through the three classic death points and ask whether the idea has a survival path through each:

1. **Death 1 — The First Belief Test**: shortly after conception, the idea encounters its first informed skeptic. Most ideas die here, dismissed as "obvious why this won't work." Survival requires either thick-skinned founders or pre-built evidence buffers.
2. **Death 2 — The Scaling Wall**: the idea works at a small scale but fails to maintain its properties as it grows (10×, 100×). Survival requires designing for scale-shift from day one — different mechanisms for different scales.
3. **Death 3 — The 1.0 → 2.0 Transition**: the idea succeeded as version 1, but the next version requires a fundamentally different approach, and the team trained on 1.0 mechanics resists. Survival requires forced re-invention triggers.

For each death point, generate ideas that BUILD IN the survival mechanism.

## Anti-mode-collapse prompts
- Do NOT skip a death point because "my idea won't face that one." Every idea faces all three. The question is which one will kill it.
- For Death 1, list the SPECIFIC objection the most informed skeptic would raise. Generic skepticism is mode collapse.
- For Death 2, the scale-shift mechanism must be DIFFERENT, not just "more of the same." If you're just adding capacity, you haven't answered the scaling question.
- For Death 3, the trigger must be self-imposed BEFORE the team gets attached to the 1.0 worldview. Reactive re-invention rarely works.

## Example application (synthetic)
Idea: "Hire engineers via 1-week paid trial projects, no resumes."

- Death 1 (first skeptic): "We can't pay candidates we won't hire — too expensive."
  - Survival: cost is offset by removing recruiting/screening burden; the trial project IS the screen.
- Death 2 (scaling wall): At 100 candidates/week, project supervision becomes overwhelming.
  - Survival: standardize trial projects so 80% can be auto-evaluated; hire a "trial coordinator" role from day 1.
- Death 3 (1.0 → 2.0): The trials become formulaic; signal degrades; new top candidates won't apply.
  - Survival: rotate trial project content every quarter via an external committee; force re-invention.

## Output format
- 3 death points × 3-5 ideas per point that build in survival mechanisms
- Tag the highest-leverage intervention point
